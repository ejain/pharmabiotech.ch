Biotechnological Applications of Lab-Chips and Microarrays

Trends in Biotechnology 2000(July);18:278-280.
Meeting Report: Cambridge Healthtech Institute's Second Annual Conference on lab-chip and microarrays held in Zuerich, Switzerland, from 17-19 January 2000.
Author & Correspondance: Prof. Kewal K. Jain

The biochip industry started using miniaturization, integration and parallel-processing techniques based on those used in the computer industry to develop laboratory devices. This technology is now revolutionizing genomics, drug development, environmental monitoring and clinical diagnostics, in much the same way as the microprocessor transformed the computer industry. The reasons underlying the success of this technology are its improved performance, higher throughput, lower cost, portability and automation. Recent advances in lab-chips and microarrays for biotechnical and biomedical applications were the focus of a recent meeting*, which covered research areas including diagnostics, microfluidics, genomic microarray systems and bioinformatics.

Diagnostic applications

Hugh Fan (Sarnoff Corporation, Princeton, NJ, USA) presented a hand-held, DNA chip-based biodetector system1, in which paramagnetic beads are coupled with microfluidics and the photodetection system is integrated in the device. Dynamic DNA hybridization was achieved for both targets and probes, indicating the potential application of this approach in gene expression analysis. A high-density DNA probe array, developed by Affymetrix (Santa Clara, CA, USA), has important applications for the identification of Mycobacterium tuberculosis species and the ability to detect of rifampicin resistance in a single platform. Alain Troesch (bioMerieux, Rockland, MA, USA) demonstrated that, by hybridizing labelled amplified genetic material from bacterial colonies to the array, this technology opens up new perspectives for the management of bacterial diseases in patients2. Elisabeth Verpoorte (University of Neuchatel, Neuchatel, Switzerland) described a multifunctional lab-on-a-chip, for automated immunoassays. Micromachined channels etched in glass enable the controlled microflow of fluids by the application of strong electric fields. It is not practical to use pumps on these devices as fluid flow is difficult to control. Chemistry and optical systems are integrated in this chip, which shows promise for automated, rapid and ultra-small volume assays.

Microfluidics

Microreaction technology offers several advantages in process engineering and biochemistry3. Wolfgang Ehrenfeld (Institut fï¿½ Mikrotechnik, Mainz, Germany) described the chemical laboratory of the future with miniaturized modules for unit operations in chemical technology. Two significant products based on this technology are: (1) the 96-channel electrophoresis chip (credit-card size); and (2) the proteome chip. Genomics lab automation is expected to be completed in the year 2000 and proteomics chips should be available by the year 2004.

The concept of polymer foundry as a key to the microfabrication of disposable microfluidic devices, was presented by Holger Becker (Jenoptik Mikrotechnik, Jena, Germany). This is a low-cost polymer-replication process that enables the mass fabrication of microstructures by Hot Embossing4. The applications of microfluidic technology include: chips for capillary electrophoresis, flow cytometry and polymerase chain reaction. In the future, nanotechnology will be used to fabricate structures with nanometer features for molecular engineering.

Anthony Rico (ACLARA Biosciences, Mountain View, CA, USA) described low-cost, plastic microfluidic systems for high-throughput screening and clinical diagnostics. The Drug Discovery LabCard(TM) has an integrated fluid transfer system, and an incubation, separation and detection in a standard 96-well format. Disposability of these cards obviates the problems of carry-over and contamination. Michael Knapp (Caliper Technologies, Mountain view, CA, USA) described the microfluidic LabChip(TM) technology for pharmaceutical screening and its genomic applications (ultra-high-throughput single nucleotide polymorphism genotyping). It can perform experiments using as little as 1-10 nanolitres of reagents and high-throughput is achieved using an assembly line approach.

Genomic microchip and microarray systems

Frederico Goodsaid (PE Biosystems, Foster City, CA, USA) described the probes designed for the TaqMan(TM) array and their application in the drug discovery process. The MicroCard Assembly (a system of fixtures) enables the TaqMan(TM) assay to be carried out in very small volumes and at reduced cost. Alex Chenchik (Clontech Laboratories, Palo Alto, CA, USA) presented the use of Atlas DNA expression arrays for high-throughput expression profiling. For tumour profiling with the Human Tumour Array, RNA is isolated from the tumour tissue as well as the control tissue, and differentially expressed genes are isolated.

Jeffrey Killian (NEN Life Science Products, Boston, MA, USA) presented Micromax(TM), a highly sensitive cDNA microarray system which uses Tyramide Signal Amplification technology that enables the detection of very small amounts of mRNA. Martin Davies (Biorobotics, Cambridge, UK) introduced the second-generation robotic microarray systems, MicroGrid Pro and MicroGrid TAS, which have been designed to address the problems that are associated with the use of current systems: (1) sample evaporation and (2) carryover. The new systems offer significantly faster processing, spotting density (as low as 10 mm), ease of use and automated bar-code reading. Their main uses are in comparative genome hybridization, gene expression profiling and single nucleotide polymorphism analysis.

Newer arrays, chips and other designs

Jonathan Cooper (University of Glasgow, Glasgow, Scotland), presented various novel, lab-on-a-chip technologies for the expression-based screening of drug leads in the drug-discovery process. These include single cell micro-biosensors, which are optical assays with three-dimensional chips and flow-through DNA chips. The significant feature of these technologies was the integration of optics and fluidics (electro-osmosis). Bernhard Weigl (Micronics, Redmond, WA, USA) described T-sensor technology for microfluidic whole-blood diagnosis (electrolytes and drug screening). Analytes can be detected by the application of optical and electrochemical methods in microvolumes of complex solutions, with internal standards to compensate for common experimental variations. Huw Davies (Ciphergen Biosystems, Surrey, UK) described the use of ProteinChip(R) arrays based on the surface enhanced laser desorption-ionisation, time-of-flight mass spectrometry technology for rapid biomarker discovery and characterization. Expression profiling with ProteinChip(R) is likely to be integrated into the proteomics laboratory of the future.

Array reading and bioinformatics

With the considerable amount of data generated by the different technologies using microarrays, it is obvious that the reading of the information and its interpretation and management through the use of bioinformatics is essential. Thus, on the final day, the lab-chip conference overlapped with the Cambridge Healthtech conference on Bioinformatics which was following the former conference. David Rank (Molecular Dynamics, Sunnyvale, CA, USA) reported on the Integrated Expressed Sequence Tag Sequencing and 'cDNA Microarray' projects. The merging of these two approaches enables the identification of novel diagnostic genes with a potential for toxicology screening. All toxicants ultimately influence gene expression and the patterns of gene expression resulting from chemical exposure are unique to a specific chemical and referred to as 'fingerprints'. A fingerprint of dioxin, a model chemical for a large class of environmental contaminants, has been developed in the mouse liver. Approximately 24 genes have been identified, the regulation of which is potentially altered following tetrachlorodibenzo-p-dioxin exposure. Further developments include: increased gene density on the toxicology chip to include 1200-1300 genes and the addition of information on other toxins. Stanley Rose (Genetic Microsystems, Woburn, MA, USA) presented novel systems that enable scientists to use microarray technologies in their own laboratories. These include 'Pin-and-Ring' spotting, the Flying Object(TM) Microscope (high-resolution scanning laser digital imaging) and transcript profiling. Finally, with so much data to be analysed, there is a need for quality control and this was the theme of presentation by Andreas Hohn (GeneData AG, Basel, Switzerland). GeneData Expressionist(TM), a technology-independent programme based on statistical analysis, can check the reproducibility and sensitivity of data, and detects problems in chip production, probe preparation and image analysis.

Conclusions

A considerable amount of information was covered during this meeting; the quality of the presentations was high and the objectives of this meeting as a forum of exchange of information and discussion were fulfilled. The importance of microarray technology and bioinformatics in the post-genomic era is obvious. These technologies will enable the study of thousands of genes simultaneously to be carried out in any laboratory.
